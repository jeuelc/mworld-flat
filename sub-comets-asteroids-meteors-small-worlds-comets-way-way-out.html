Where do comets come from? If they are gradually boiling away under the sun's glare, why are some of them still around the solar system has been around for billions of years, why haven't they boiled away?

In 1950 astronomer Jan Oort realised there must be a whole lot of comets in the cold outer parts of the solar system. They stay there for billions of years, remaining frozen solid.

Then a collision with another comet, meteoroid or the stronger gravity of a planet, throws some of these comets towards the sun. The comets gradually boil away each time they approach the sun.

Some comets orbit the sun at least once a century. They are 'short period' comets and they come from the 'Kuiper Belt'. This area starts just beyond Neptune. Have you heard of Pluto? Pluto is one of the biggest objects in the Kuiper Belt.

Some comets take more than a century to complete one orbit around the sun. These 'long period' comets usually come from the Oort Cloud. This is a vast round cloud that is over a trillion kilometres from the sun! If you were that far away, the sun would look like a very bright star.
