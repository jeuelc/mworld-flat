People have always wondered what comets were. They appear in the sky for days or sometimes weeks, or even months! They look like fuzzy stars, sometimes with tails streaming far behind them. Most comets are faint and hard to see, but sometimes a comet can be bright and its spectacular tail can stretch across the sky!

In the past some people thought they brought good luck, other people thought they meant disaster was about to happen! People wanted to know if they were just above the clouds, wandering between the planets, or up among the stars?

Edmund Halley solved part of the problem in 1705. He thought that comets that had been seen in 1456, 1531, 1607 and 1682 were actually the same comet, and predicted that it would return in 1758. On the 25 December 1758, the comet returned proving that his prediction was correct! This proved that comets circle the sun, travelling on orbits that take them from the farthest parts of the solar system in to the inner solar system and the planets.

What are they made of? Why are they fuzzy? Why do they have tails? Fred Whipple figured this out in 1950. Comets are giant dirty snowballs, perhaps kilometres in size. As these comets get nearer to the sun, the heat from the Sun boils away some of the ice, producing a tail of gas, ice crystals and dust that can stretch for millions of kilometres!
