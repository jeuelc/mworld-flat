Currents move through the ocean; they're a bit like underwater rivers. But did you know there are some that go from the top to the bottom too? Surface currents are found in the top 400 metres, which is still pretty deep! It is mainly the wind that drives the surface currents. Most ocean currents are way down below 400 metres; they are the deep currents. Did you know that currents in the southern hemisphere move slower than in the northern hemisphere? And that currents turn in different directions in the two hemispheres?

All around the world the sea rises and falls twice a day, every day! This rise and fall is called a tide. When the tide rises the sea moves upwards and inland and when it falls the sea sinks back. This is why sometimes when you go to the beach you can play in rock pools and other times the rock pools are underwater. But why does this happen? What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!
