Tropical cyclones

 
click here click here

Tropical cyclones are huge, spinning storms hundreds of kilometres across. In some places, people call them typhoons or hurricanes.

Tropical cyclones only happen in the tropics where ocean waters are relatively warm (about 26.5°C or above). They start when a lot of thunderstorms join together. Using moisture from the warm ocean surface as fuel, these storms grow. If they are more than 500 kilometres from the equator, the joined storms start to spin and a tropical cyclone is born.

As tropical cyclones 'spin up' they can become dangerous. If they hit land, very strong winds (sometimes over 250 kilometres per hour!) can knock over trees and buildings, and the heavy rains cause flooding. The strong winds also cause the ocean to rise up, called a storm surge, which brings with it large waves that crash into the land.

But once a tropical cyclone hits land, its fate has been sealed. It can't keep going for long because its energy supply, the warm ocean waters, are gone.

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!
