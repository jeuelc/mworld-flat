Supercells and tornadoes

 

When a regular thunderstorm is given a couple of extra ingredients it becomes super-big and bad. We call it a supercell thunderstorm!

The first ingredient is a lot of warm and moist air so that the supercell has lots of fuel.

The second ingredient is that the winds at the ground need to blow in a different direction to the winds higher in the sky, which makes the supercell spin slowly.

These extra ingredients mean supercells have lots of fast-moving air that spirals upwards through the storm and makes hail the size of a baseball or cricket ball! The air spins faster and faster until it turns into a 'vortex', which looks like water when you take the plug out of a bathtub. This vortex usually stays in the clouds, but sometimes it gets so strong that it reaches to the ground and when it does, it's called a tornado!

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!
